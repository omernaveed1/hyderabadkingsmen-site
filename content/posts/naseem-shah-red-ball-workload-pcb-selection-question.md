Naseem Shah is 21 years old, bowls at 145 kph, and is the most naturally gifted fast bowler Pakistan has produced since Shoaib Akhtar in his pomp. He is also, quietly, becoming the PCB's biggest selection headache — not because of poor form or attitude, but because of the sheer fragility that tends to accompany bowlers who generate this much pace at this age.

## The Real Question Behind the Selections

On the surface, every conversation about Naseem Shah seems to revolve around fitness bulletins and injury updates. That framing misses the deeper issue. The PCB is not simply managing a recurring injury. It is trying to decide what kind of cricketer Naseem Shah should be across all three formats over the next decade — and those decisions, made right now in his early twenties, will define whether Pakistan has a genuine red-ball spearhead through the 2030s or a brilliant white-ball asset who fades from Test cricket by thirty.

Fast bowlers who bowl flat-out in T20 leagues, then turn around and carry Test attacks without adequate recovery, have a well-documented history of breaking down. Pakistan's own recent past — think Mohammad Amir's second coming, think Hasan Ali's fluctuating Test availability — offers cautionary evidence. The PCB has seen this cycle before. The question is whether it has truly learned from it.

Naseem is not just any young quick. He announced himself in Test cricket as a teenager, swinging the ball both ways in conditions that would challenge veterans. His ability to generate outswing at genuine pace is a rare gift. Red-ball cricket, in many ways, is where that gift finds its fullest expression. Yet the volume of white-ball cricket — PSL, bilateral T20Is, ICC tournaments — constantly competes for his body's finite resources.

## The Workload Arithmetic

A standard Test match asks a frontline pacer for somewhere between 25 and 40 overs across five days. A typical PSL campaign, factoring in travel and back-to-back fixtures, is its own kind of grind even if individual spells are shorter. Stack a home series, a PSL season, an overseas tour, and a global tournament into the same twelve-month block and the arithmetic becomes alarming.

The PCB's selection committee and team management need to do something genuinely difficult: say no to Naseem in certain formats, at certain times, even when he is fit and available and eager. That requires political will that boards frequently talk about but rarely exercise — especially when a player's presence can shift ticket sales and television ratings.

There is also the question of what Test cricket needs from him specifically. Pakistan's pace attack in the longest format has often leaned heavily on one bowler carrying the load. Shaheen Shah Afridi has been that bowler in recent years. Having Naseem as a fully loaded, match-fit second spearhead — rather than a half-fit option shoehorned in after a white-ball tournament — changes Pakistan's Test XI in ways that cannot be overstated.

## What a Sensible Plan Looks Like

A workload plan with teeth would prioritise Test availability in home series and away tours against top-ranked sides. It would treat certain T20I assignments as optional rather than compulsory, and it would involve honest communication with PSL franchises about the number of matches Naseem can realistically play without compromising his four-day game.

This is not a radical idea. England rotated James Anderson and Stuart Broad for years. Australia carefully managed Pat Cummins and Mitchell Starc through white-ball cycles before both became the backbone of a Test revival. The model exists. It just requires a board willing to treat its own long-term interest above short-term box-office demands.

## What to Watch Next

The next six months will be telling. How the PCB handles Naseem's availability for upcoming Test assignments, against the backdrop of franchise commitments, will signal whether the board has a genuine fast-bowling strategy or whether it is still making it up as it goes along. Watch, specifically, whether he is given any scheduled rest windows between formats or whether he is expected to be available for every squad across every format.

Naseem Shah has already shown he belongs at the highest level of Test cricket. The PCB's job now is to make sure he is still there in 2030. That means making the hard calls now, while there is still time to get it right.
