When Sunrisers Hyderabad posted 287 for 3 against Royal Challengers Bengaluru in March 2024, it was filed away as a one-off. A flat Chinnaswamy track, two Australians in red-hot form, and a bowling attack having a bad night. Two seasons on, that scoreboard is doing something stranger: it is setting the par.

## The number that broke a ceiling

For seventeen IPL seasons, 240 was the ceiling that decent teams pushed against and great ones occasionally cleared. Travis Head and Heinrich Klaasen broke through that ceiling so casually on March 25, 2024 that the league briefly forgot how to react. Head made 102 off 41. Klaasen made 67 off 31. Abhishek Sharma chipped in with 34 off 12. RCB chased 288 hard and still lost by 25 runs, with Dinesh Karthik playing a knock that would have headlined any other match.

What that night did was give every IPL batting unit a new mental anchor. If 287 is gettable, 220 stops being a winning total. 240 stops being a great one. The shift was psychological more than tactical, and it stuck.

## What changed inside SRH

The SRH dressing room of 2024 was an experiment in permission. Brian Lara, in his first year as head coach, kept telling the top five to "bat without arithmetic." No scoreboard math in the middle overs. No saving wickets for the death. The numbers that came back were absurd: an SRH powerplay strike rate of 188, a middle-overs strike rate that for the first time in IPL history exceeded the powerplay's, and a death-overs run rate of 14 an over across the season.

You can argue Lara borrowed the doctrine from Brendon McCullum's Bazball, but the cricket is honestly distinct. McCullum told batters to chase counter-attack as a default. Lara told his top five to ignore the bowler entirely and bat to the ground, the wind, and their own hands. That is why SRH could put up 287 at Chinnaswamy and 277 at Kolkata in the same fortnight.

## Why the 287 still matters in 2026

Look at the par scores across the first half of IPL 2026. Mumbai Indians chased 257 at Wankhede. CSK posted 268 at Chepauk, a venue where 180 used to be a winning score. Punjab Kings, of all teams, defended 261. The league is now playing at the tempo SRH set in 2024.

The teams who have not adjusted are the teams losing. Royal Challengers Bengaluru, despite Kohli's continued brilliance, have been the league's slowest-starting unit in the powerplay this season, and it is costing them games they should be winning. Lucknow Super Giants are still running their innings on 2022 maths and have an NRR of -0.43 to show for it.

SRH's 287 was not the highest IPL total it will ever be. RCB themselves cleared 300 a year later. But it was the moment the league's idea of what counted as a big score broke, and we are still living inside that break.

## Player to remember

[Heinrich Klaasen](/players/heinrich-klaasen/), who came in at 167 for 2 in the tenth over and proceeded to hit five sixes inside two and a half overs. The runs got Travis Head the headlines because his hundred came first, but it was Klaasen's middle-overs detonation that pushed the score from "very good" to "historic." He has spent the eighteen months since being the highest-impact middle-order batter in T20 cricket, and the Chinnaswamy night is the moment he claimed that crown.

## What it means for the rest of IPL 2026

If you are following SRH this season, watch for two things. First, whether the team's new captaincy under Pat Cummins changes the tempo. Cummins is more conservative than Aiden Markram was at the helm in 2024 and may dial down the foot-on-throat doctrine. Second, whether Klaasen's body holds up. He has played non-stop white-ball cricket since 2023 and there were small signs of a slower bat in last week's loss at Eden Gardens. SRH's whole batting model depends on him being healthy in May.

The 287 stands as the moment a franchise rewrote a league's idea of itself. Few innings in IPL history have done that. The legacy is everywhere you look this season.
