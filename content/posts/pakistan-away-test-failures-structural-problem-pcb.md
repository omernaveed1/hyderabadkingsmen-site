Pakistan's away Test record over the past decade is not a slump. It is a pattern, and patterns have causes. Until the PCB treats this as an institutional problem rather than a selection inconvenience, the results will not change.

## The Numbers Tell a Damning Story

Cast your eye across Pakistan's away Test campaigns in recent years and a grim picture emerges. Series defeats in England, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa and the subcontinent away from home in Sri Lanka. The odd series win in Zimbabwe or the West Indies is used to paper over the cracks, but those results flatter a side that is genuinely struggling to compete on foreign soil against the top eight.

The instinct at the PCB is to change the captain, shuffle the selectors, or blame the pitch curators in the host country. The structural causes, the ones that persist regardless of who is wearing the armband, are rarely confronted.

## What This Story Is Really About

The core problem is preparation, and it starts with domestic cricket.

Pakistan's first-class structure, the Quaid-e-Azam Trophy, produces excellent batters on flat, dry Subcontinental pitches. That is fine for home conditions. It is a disaster for producing cricketers who can handle lateral movement, carry-and-bounce, or the seaming ball under overhead conditions. A young batter from Karachi or Lahore can go through an entire first-class career without facing the kind of delivery that James Anderson or Pat Cummins will bowl at him on day one of a Test abroad.

Contrast this with England's county circuit, where even a second-division match in April will demand technique against seam and swing. Or Australia's Sheffield Shield, which is explicitly designed to replicate the pace and bounce of Test arenas. Pakistan's domestic structure is not designed to prepare players for away Tests. It is designed to fill a schedule.

The second structural failure is tour preparation. Pakistan routinely arrive at away Test series having played minimal first-class cricket in the preceding weeks. The county season or domestic warm-ups are either skipped or treated as irrelevant by an administration more focused on bilateral white-ball commitments. By the time the Test series begins, Pakistan's batters are playing their first red-ball cricket in conditions they do not understand, against bowlers who do.

Third, and perhaps most insidious, is the prioritisation of T20 cricket at the expense of Test skills development. The PSL is a commercial success and a brilliant competition, but it has consequences. Young cricketers are shaped by the incentive structure around them. When a franchise T20 contract is worth more than a full domestic red-ball season, the message to a teenager is clear: power hitting and death bowling matter more than leaving the ball outside off stump.

## What It Means Going Forward

For Babar Azam and whoever leads Pakistan's Test side in the near term, this structural deficit means they are always fighting uphill. Individual brilliance can paper over systemic failure for one series, maybe two. But series wins in England or Australia require an entire batting lineup that has been forged in the right conditions, against the right bowling, over years. Pakistan simply does not have the pipeline that produces those players consistently.

The PCB has occasionally gestured toward reform. Pitches in domestic cricket have been made slightly more sporting in some centres. But gestures are not policy, and policy without enforcement means nothing. Without a deliberate, multi-year plan to restructure domestic pitches, extend first-class seasons, and make red-ball cricket financially attractive to young players, the same conversations will be had after the next away series defeat.

There is also the question of coaching continuity. Pakistan's Test coaching setup changes with alarming regularity, making it almost impossible to build and communicate a coherent technical philosophy across the squad.

## What to Watch Next

Keep an eye on Pakistan's next away Test assignment, specifically on the preparation window the PCB grants the squad beforehand. Watch how many red-ball warm-up games are played, whether frontline batters are rested or rotated for white-ball commitments in the weeks prior, and whether any domestic pitches in Pakistan actually deteriorate over five days the way Test pitches abroad do.

Those small details will tell you far more about whether the PCB is serious than any press conference statement will.

The talent in Pakistani cricket is not in question. It never has been. The question is whether the institution responsible for developing that talent is willing to make uncomfortable structural choices. History, unfortunately, suggests it is not.
