Heinrich Klaasen plays a kind of T20 cricket that did not exist five years ago. He is a wicketkeeper-batter from Pretoria who came up through South Africa's domestic system as a competent middle-order accumulator and, somewhere between 2022 and 2024, turned himself into the most violent middle-overs hitter in the world.

## How he became the SRH detonator

Klaasen joined SRH in 2023 after the franchise lost David Warner and needed a six-hitter who could anchor the middle order. The fit was awkward at first. He was used at number four, then five, then promoted to three. None of it quite clicked until Brian Lara took over as head coach in late 2023 and told him to bat at five with a single instruction: "When you arrive, the tempo is yours."

What that meant in practice was permission to attack from ball one of his innings, regardless of match situation. Klaasen had never been allowed to do that for South Africa or in the SA20. He took to it in a way that surprised even him. By the end of IPL 2024, he had hit 31 sixes in 11 innings — a rate that no middle-order batter had ever sustained in the format.

## Recent form

2025 was quieter. Klaasen averaged 36 in IPL 2025 with a strike rate of 152, which is still elite but a clear step down from 2024. The drop came from two things: opposition teams started bowling him heavier lengths from the very first ball, denying him room to free his arms; and he was clearly fatigued from a non-stop year of cricket across the IPL, SA20, ILT20 and South Africa duty.

The first half of IPL 2026 has been better. He has three fifties in seven innings at a strike rate of 168, and the Wankhede knock against Mumbai (78 not out off 34) was the kind of innings only he plays. SRH have been careful with his workload, holding him back from a couple of warm-up games to keep him fresh for the back half of the season.

## What to watch

Two things define Klaasen's season from here. First, whether SRH continue to use him at five rather than slide him up. Pat Cummins as captain has been more conservative about the batting order than Aiden Markram was, and there have been murmurs about a move to four. That would change his role from detonator to anchor, and the numbers say it would cost the team. Second, his white-ball-cricket schedule between IPL and the T20 World Cup. If he plays all of it, he arrives at the World Cup tired. If South Africa rest him, SRH should benefit from a sharper Klaasen in the playoffs.

## What makes him different

Most six-hitters in T20 cricket have a clear arc — off-side, leg-side, or down the ground — that bowlers can deny. Klaasen's arc is the whole 360. His back-foot pull over deep midwicket, his slog-sweep, his lofted drive over extra cover, and his uppercut over third man are all played from the same setup. That is why bowlers cannot box him in. There is no obvious place to bowl.

If you are watching SRH this season and a new viewer asks you "which player should I focus on?", the answer is Klaasen. Travis Head will hit the headlines. Pat Cummins will get the captaincy questions. But the player who decides whether SRH win the trophy is the South African at number five.
